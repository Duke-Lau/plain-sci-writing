# Plainness Checklist

Use this checklist when the text needs an explicit "human writing" audit. Do not apply it mechanically; preserve the author's intended meaning and journal register.

## Paragraph logic

- The first sentence gives the reader a reason to continue.
- The paragraph has one central job, not three loosely related jobs.
- The claim appears before interpretation when reporting results.
- The sentence order follows evidence before meaning, or known before unknown.
- The final sentence either closes the point or sets up the next point.
- No paragraph relies on vague phrases such as "this issue", "this aspect", or "this field" when a concrete noun would be clearer.

## Common AI-tone signals

- Inflated framing: "plays a vital role", "is of great significance", "has attracted increasing attention".
- Empty contribution language: "provides new insights", "offers a theoretical basis", "has important implications" without naming the insight, basis, or implication.
- Over-balanced templates: "not only...but also", "with the rapid development of", "in today's society", "from multiple perspectives".
- Decorative transitions: "furthermore", "moreover", and "in addition" used between weakly connected claims.
- Dense abstraction chains: "the optimization of the construction of the mechanism of..." where verbs and concrete nouns would be clearer.
- Repeated sentence shapes that make the paragraph sound generated.

## SCI plainness rules

- Replace "important" with the specific importance: prevalence, cost, uncertainty, mechanism, practice gap, policy need, or methodological limitation.
- Replace "significant" with "statistically significant" only when a test supports it; otherwise use "substantial", "clear", "larger", "smaller", or a quantitative estimate.
- Replace "novel" with the actual difference from prior work unless novelty has been established.
- Replace "explore" with the actual action when possible: examine, estimate, compare, test, characterize, code, classify, or model.
- Keep cautious verbs when the design is observational: suggest, are associated with, are consistent with, may reflect.
- Avoid causal verbs unless the design supports causal inference.

## Results versus Discussion

Results sentences should answer:

- What was measured?
- In whom or what?
- Compared with what?
- How large was the difference or association?
- What uncertainty or statistical evidence was reported?

Discussion sentences should answer:

- What does the result mean?
- How does it fit or conflict with prior work?
- Why might this pattern occur?
- What are the limits of that interpretation?
- What should readers do with the finding?

If a paragraph mixes both, separate the observation from the interpretation before polishing.

## Chinese draft repair

For Chinese academic drafts:

- First reduce long "背景-意义-重要性" openings to one concrete reason.
- Convert nominalized phrases into actor-action-result logic.
- Keep discipline-specific terms, but remove duplicate near-synonyms.
- Resolve pronouns and broad nouns before translation.
- If translating, translate the repaired idea, not the original sentence order.

## Final self-check

Before returning the revision, verify:

- No new fact, reference, mechanism, sample description, or statistical result was added.
- The main claim is easier to identify than in the source text.
- The prose is simpler but not casual.
- Technical meaning is preserved.
- Overclaims are softened rather than disguised.
